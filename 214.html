What's amazing about this story is that nobody's mad. Keith's not bitter that he had no access to his son. David's not mad at his mother and never has been. Even Dave Senior isn't angry. He still feels just as much David's father as he ever did.

Which brings us back to Occam's Razor, the principle that the simplest explanation for something is the best one. In a family, the explanation that seem simplest can switch over time. When David was young, the simpler thing to believe was that his parents were his parents, and his family was his family, and that he was a Paladino. As he got older, that story got harder to maintain. And the other explanation, that Keith was his father, became the simplest for everyone.

And when the family shifted from one story to the other, they didn't get caught up in issues of race and paternity and lying and betrayal. When the truth came out, the truth that the whole family was fearing for so long, it didn't spark any great trauma at all. It just put people at peace.

Act Two. The Trajectory And Force Of Bodies In Orbit.

Jon Ronson

My family runs a hotel in the Brecon Beacons mountain range of mid-Wales. I go home to the hotel every Christmas. And at some point, I always manage to say, &quot;I treat this place just like a hotel.&quot; And this always gets a big laugh.

Sometimes, famous people stay at the hotel, too, which my parents treat as a perk of the job. My mother always calls me up when it happens. When the British TV news reporter, John Cole was there recently, my mother phoned me up and said, &quot;Guess what? John Cole's staying here. Oh, and he hasn't heard of you.&quot;

A few years ago, John Birt, the Director General of the BBC, came in to have lunch. My father approached his table. &quot;Are you John Birt?&quot; he asked. &quot;Yes,&quot; said John Birt. &quot;I wonder if you can help,&quot; said my father. &quot;The TV reception in this area can be all crackly and fuzzy. Is there anything you can do about this?&quot; I think my father wanted John Birt to get onto the roof and fix the aerial.

&quot;We spoke about all sorts,&quot; my father told me on the phone afterwards. &quot;The problem I'm having with my car. He couldn't believe it's been in the garage six times.&quot; &quot;Oh, and he hasn't heard of you,&quot; I did my mother on the extension line.

Recently, my parents won the coveted Welsh Hotel of the Year competition. It was a big honor. And they wanted to do something to commemorate the success. So they decided to commission a portrait painter to immortalize the Ronsons.

&quot;We've decided to have a group family portrait commissioned,&quot; said my mother on the phone, &quot;a Ronson family portrait to be hung in the bar. Will you be available for a sitting?&quot; &quot;Certainly,&quot; I said. &quot;Who's doing it?&quot;

&quot;He's a brilliant but troubled local artist,&quot; she said. &quot;He did the mural for the new Cardiff multiplex. You must have heard of it.&quot; &quot;No,&quot; I said. &quot;Oh, come on,&quot; she said. &quot;It's been in all the papers. Anyway he paints hotels, too. He came up to ask us if we wanted ours painted.&quot;

&quot;You mean he depicts hotels on canvas?&quot; I asked. &quot;Oh, no, no,&quot; she said. &quot;He actually paints the walls of hotels.&quot; &quot;So he's a painter and a decorator?&quot; I asked. &quot;What's your problem,&quot; said my mother. &quot;Are you being snooty?&quot;

It turned out that the brilliant but troubled artist's particular sub-specialty is painting celebrities in classical Renaissance settings, like Clint Eastwood ascending to heaven surrounded by angels. His loving re-creations of celebrities set my parents thinking. So many famous people stay at their hotel, what if the Ronson family portrait was extended to include celebrities?

&quot;Listen to this,&quot; said my mother on the phone the next day. &quot;We, the family, will be standing in the grounds of the hotel, surrounded by famous people.&quot; &quot;Which famous people?&quot; I asked. &quot;You mean the famous people who've stayed at the hotel?&quot; &quot;Oh, no,&quot; said my mother, &quot;Any famous people that we choose.&quot;

I fell silent on the other hand of the phone, trying to let the incredibleness of this proposed project sink in. My mother carried on. She said, &quot;We're working on a tight deadline. Send a Polaroid of yourself to the artist. You have to come up with your three famous people by Wednesday, living or dead, comedians, statesman, actors, anything.&quot;

&quot;Let me clarify this in my head,&quot; I said. &quot;Thre's nothing to clarify,&quot; said my mother. &quot;Don't you think it may come across as a little self-aggrandizing?&quot; I asked. &quot;I'm choosing President Kennedy, Gandhi, and Churchill,&quot; said my mother.

My father came onto the phone. &quot;Who are you choosing?&quot; I asked him. &quot;Gary Player, Arnold Palmer, and Jack Nicklaus,&quot; he said. &quot;All golfers?&quot; I asked. &quot;Who are you choosing?&quot; asked my father.

My mind drew a blank. In fact, I began to panic. I imagined in hundreds of years time, notable art historians gathered around the painting, making sarcastic comments. Paintings are so permanent. I was to be frozen in time for eternity with three celebrities. And now the question was, which celebrities best represented Jon Ronson's essence?

It was easy for my father. It was as if each of his three golfers portrayed a different subtlety to his personality. There was Arnold Palmer, the kind and thoughtful golfer with the common touch. There was Jack Nicklaus, the fiery, steely golfer, who once said, &quot;Nobody ever remembers who finished second at anything.&quot; And there was Gary Player, the philosophical golfer, whose 10 commandments for life, as seen on garyplayer.com, includes &quot;The fox fears not the man who boasts by night, but the man who rises early in the morning.&quot;

But who could I choose? I found myself feeling hostile to the whole idea, a hostility that manifested itself in a lazy choice of celebrities. &quot;I'm going for the Beverly sisters,&quot; I told my mother on the phone.

I actually have no interest in girl groups of the 1940s, but I did know that the Beverly sisters all looked exactly alike. And my choice was designed to be viewed by art historians of the future as an ironic, silent protest. &quot;You can't have the Beverly sisters,&quot; said my mother, knowing me well enough to understand all of this in an instant. &quot;How about Sister Sledge?&quot; I said. &quot;Are you trying to ruin this?&quot; said my mother.

I relented and opted for my real, all-time celebrity hero. &quot;Randy Newman,&quot; I said. &quot;Nobody knows what Randy Newman looks like,&quot; she snapped. In the end, we compromised. For all time, it would be Jon Ronson, Ike and Tina Turner, and Boris Yeltsin.

The Ronsons were going to be hobnobbing amongst the stars. The laws of physics tells us that one needs to be very careful approaching a star. If your trajectory and speed are just right, you'll go into orbit, safe and sound, glowing in the warm, beautiful starlight. If you mess up in any way, the wrong direction, the wrong speed, you'll hurtle into the face of the star and be vaporized, and only the star will remain. My parents were involving us in a very dangerous game.

A few days later, my mother called to tell me that the concept had slightly changed. Each Ronson, she said, would no longer be just standing and chatting to the celebrities. We were to be serving them drinks. This was, after all, a hotel. So now I was to be frozen in time in an act of subservience to Boris Yeltsin.

A few days later, the concept changed yet again. It was back to standing and chatting, but this time, the Ronsons would be talking, and the celebrities would be listening. &quot;So you'll be talking,&quot; I clarified, &quot;and Kennedy, Gandhi, and Churchill will be listening.&quot; &quot;What's the problem?&quot; said my mother. &quot;Oh, nothing, nothing,&quot; I said. &quot;I'm just worried that people may get the wrong idea, that we consider ourselves as good as Kennedy and--&quot; &quot;What are you saying?&quot; said my mother. &quot;Nothing,&quot; I said.

I changed the subject. &quot;What will you be talking to Kennedy, Gandhi, and Churchill about?&quot; &quot;What do you mean, what will I be talking to them about?&quot; said my mother. &quot;Nothing. I'm talking to them about nothing. It's a painting.&quot; &quot;Maybe Dad would be talking to the golfers about the amount of times his car's been in the garage,&quot; I suggested.

I sent in my Polaroids, and we all waited. Months passed. My father would telephone the artist from time to time to find out how things were going, and when was he going to be finished. Most often, the artist didn't pick up. And when he did, he was a man of few words, words that were spoken gruffly.

&quot;Nearly there,&quot; he said. &quot;Are you pleased with it so far?&quot; asked my father. &quot;Don't worry,&quot; said the artist. My father began to worry.

The day of the grand unveiling came without warning. The artist just turned up one morning, carrying a large canvas covered in a white sheet. He propped it up against the bar.

The family gathered around it with a sense of great expectation. Everyone looked at the covered painting and at the brilliant but troubled artist, trying to scrutinize his facial expression. It was, well, troubled.

The artist said, &quot;I think you ought to know that I'm going through a creative stage which some people find difficult to connect to.&quot; There was a nervous silence. &quot;What I'm saying,&quot; he continued, &quot;is there's a possibility you may not like it.&quot;

The Ronsons looked anxiously at one another. Then, with a flourish, the artist whipped off the sheet. &quot;There you go,&quot; he said.

For a moment, the Ronsons just stared. My mother whispered, &quot;Oh my God.&quot; She clutched her chest, yelped quietly, then stormed out of the room. My brother and his wife followed as a show of unity, slamming the door behind them.

The artist was left alone with my father. They didn't make eye contact. They just stared at the painting.

The famous people had all been painted with tender accuracy. There were a few celebrities, like Clint Eastwood, that none of the Ronsons had actually asked for. But that wasn't the problem. The problem was that although the celebrities were lovingly depicted, the Ronsons stood amongst them as human grotesqueries, repulsive caricatures of monsters.

My brother looked like Frankenstein. He had a bolt through his neck. It was disturbing and humiliating. My parents looked like hastily sketched recovering drug addicts. I looked like a gawky, spotty adolescent frozen in a gormless pose.

&quot;I'm sorry,&quot; said the painter, looking at the floor. &quot;We're not paying for it,&quot; said my father. There was a long silence. My father ordered the artist to paint the Ronsons out. And after much negotiation, he agreed.

He turned my mother into Woody Allen. That's kind of humiliating in itself, that the easiest brush-over would render her as Woody Allen. My father was Jimmy Carter. I like to think the artist chose Jimmy Carter, the famed peacemaker, to replace my dad in an homage to my father's diplomacy, as he was the only Ronson who didn't storm out. My brother was turned into David Rockefeller, and his wife was turned into Henry Kissinger, which I interpret as an act of hostility. The only Ronson left in the portrait is, oddly, me.

The painting now hangs between the cigarette machine and the coat stand of the bar in my family's hotel, and it's the cause of much interest amongst the customers. They crowd around it, trying to guess who everyone is. &quot;That's Kennedy,&quot; they say. &quot;Look, Boris Yeltsin.&quot; Nobody gets David Rockefeller. I have to say, &quot;It's David Rockefeller.&quot; &quot;Ah,&quot; they reply. &quot;David Rockefeller.&quot;

They stare blankly at the likeness of me, too, for whole minutes at a time, trying to figure out who exactly I am. &quot;I'm famous,&quot; I say. &quot;I've been on TV.&quot; But they all just squint and shrug. If my mother's there, she'll cheerfully pipe in, &quot;Oh, they've never heard of you.&quot;
